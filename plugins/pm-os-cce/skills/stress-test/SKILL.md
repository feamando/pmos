# Stress Test

Structured adversarial review — extract assumptions, research counters, rate confidence, recommend.

## When to Apply

- User explicitly asks to "stress-test", "challenge", "validate", or "poke holes" in something
- User shares a plan, claim, or decision and asks for critical feedback
- Before committing to a major decision or publishing a business case

## Input Types

- **A claim:** "Feature X reduces cancellations by 2%"
- **A plan:** "We should roll out to 100% next week"
- **A document section:** "Stress-test the business case assumptions"
- **A decision:** "We're going with approach A over B"

If no specific input is provided, ask: "What would you like me to stress-test?"

## Protocol

### Step 1: Extract Assumptions

List every assumption — stated and unstated — that the claim/plan/decision rests on. Be exhaustive:
- Data assumptions (sample size, date range, settled vs unsettled, metric definitions)
- Causal assumptions (X causes Y, not just correlates)
- Scope assumptions (applies to this market/segment/timeframe)
- Business assumptions (engagement will grow, costs stay flat, no competitor response)
- Technical assumptions (tracking works, no bugs, pipeline is reliable)

Number each assumption.

### Step 2: Research Counters

For each assumption, research and present:
- **The counter-argument:** What's the strongest reason this assumption could be wrong?
- **Evidence:** Is there data, prior experience, or external precedent that challenges it?
- **Impact if wrong:** What happens to the overall conclusion if this assumption breaks?

Do actual research — check Brain, read docs, look at data. Don't just brainstorm hypothetical objections.

### Step 3: Rate Confidence

Rate each assumption:
- **High (8-10):** Strong evidence supports it, counter-arguments are weak
- **Medium (5-7):** Plausible but unverified, or evidence is mixed
- **Low (1-4):** Weak evidence, strong counter-arguments, or untested

### Step 4: Present

Format as a table:

| # | Assumption | Confidence | Key Risk | Impact if Wrong |
|---|-----------|-----------|----------|----------------|

Then summarize:
- **Strongest assumptions** (highest confidence, lowest risk)
- **Weakest links** (lowest confidence, highest impact)
- **What would change your mind** — specific observations or data that would invalidate the conclusion
- **Recommendation:** Proceed / Proceed with caution / Reconsider

## Rules

- Be genuinely adversarial. The value is in finding real weaknesses, not validating the user's thinking.
- Don't soften the conclusions. If something is weak, say it's weak.
- Reference actual data where available — don't just theorize.
- Apply intellectual honesty: no anchor-adjusting, no unsupported claims.
- If the user pushes back, engage with their counter — don't back down without new evidence.
